US Hate Crimes: 2005 - 2012

October 27, 2013

Open in a new window.

Intro

This map shows the distribution of hate crimes across the US from 2005 to 2012. Data is derived from FBI hate crime statistics published each year, and US census data. The map is rendered using D3.js using the Albers projection. Coloring is based off the number of hate crimes committed in each state normalized based off the population of the state.

Data Aggregation

Building the Map

Hate Crime Distribution

The colors are derived from a linear distribution based off per capita data for all years of the data set. By scaling based off all years the data is able to show year to year change, rather than just state to state within a year.

Map Bias

Distributions are bias toward low population states. Since the data determines per capita rates, singular crimes in low population states have greater impact on the per capita crime rate. Those states with small populations that have high tourism industries are effected even more since crimes against or by non-residents also impact crime rates. This effect especially impacts states such as D.C. and Hawaii.

Challenges

  • State Naming

    • Resolving state names from FBI data, especially two work names, to state abbreviations for TopoJSON referencing.

  • Data Irregularities

    • Dealing with states with no crimes that year

Future Development

  • Bayesian Distribution

    • Base coloring based on Bayesian distribution. Visualize states either far above or below the mean. State below specifically are under visualized currently. Consistently low numbers of hate crimes could allude to bad enforcement of federal hate crime legislation.

  • Legislation Analysis

    • Currently this map is just a basic analysis of known hate crimes across the US. Development is being done to compare trends against legislation passed regarding hate crime. The final goal is to determine what type of legislation and enforcement effect hate crimes the most. A further objective is to determine which states need to improve their legislation or enforcement procedures the most.
